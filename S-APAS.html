    <p dir="ltr"><span style="font-size:19px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Rising sea levels will eventually make some home unlivable</span></span><br />

    &nbsp;</p>

    <strong><span style="font-size:26px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Experts say Orange County must speed adaptation strategies for ocean’s encroachment</span></span></strong><br />

    <br />

    <span style="font-size:16px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">By John Orkun<br />

    <em>Orange County Register</em></span></span><br />

    <br />

    <span style="font-size:19px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">How much adaptation planning officials pursue, starting now, will determine whether several Orange County, CA, coastal communities will suffer damage and increased costs from ocean flooding, experts say. Adaption addresses ways a community can prepare for impact of sea-level rise.<br />

    <br />

    On Newport Beach’s Balboa Island, for instance, during especially high tides, water from the bay burbles up through cracks in the sidewalk and laps at the top of 80-year-old sea walls, which loom several feet above lower-lying walkways.<br />

    <br />

    But this occasional nuisance will become increasingly common in several Orange County coastal communities, scientists say, unless essential flood-resilience plans are put in place.<br />

    <br />

    Low-elevation coastal areas such as Balboa, the Newport Peninsula, Sunset Beach and Seal Beach will feel the effects first. Gradually, over decades, according to researchers, ankle-deep floods will become knee-deep, waist-deep and eventually strong enough to wash away cars and make some homes unlivable.<br />

    <br />

    “Something that wouldn’t have been a big flood previously will become a much more significant flood,” said Timu Gallien of the Scripps Institution of Oceanography in San Diego.<br />

    <br />

    Most coastal Orange County cities are beginning to act on necessary adaptation planning by incorporating sea level rise projections into planning documents and general plan updates. <br />

    <br />

    It can take years to prepare flood studies that provide detailed information on neighborhood-level vulnerabilities. Sea walls, dikes and beach sand replenishment projects cost millions of dollars and are extremely time-consuming to plan, approve and build.<br />

    <br />

    But some resilience work is moving ahead. In Newport Beach, a city expected to face some of the most severe consequences, officials recently settled on a $2 million compromise plan to raise sea walls protecting flood-prone areas by nine inches, after some homeowners objected to higher walls that could interfere with their bay views, said Bob Stein, the assistant city engineer.<br />

    <br />

    Huntington Beach, another city in Orange County facing sea level rise, has convened an adaptation task force to work on a coastal resiliency plan that will go into the city’s general plan update in the coming years. Seal Beach and Dana Point also are taking preliminary steps to assess how to adapt if rising seas affect homes, businesses and roads.<br />

    <br />

    The first, enhanced defenses against rising sea levels, she said, should be natural adaptive methods, such as planting oyster beds and mangrove forests that can weaken the strongest tides and slow the advance of waves. Building engineered sea walls and tide gates would come next, followed eventually by pulling back development.<br />

    <br />

    The danger with failing cliffs is that even though the danger builds gradually, the damage crashes down all at once. <br />

    <br />

    “You can’t predict when it’s going to happen,” he said.</span></span>
