    <strong><span style="font-size:26px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Adaptation on the agenda as Missouri grapples with hotter future</span></span></strong><br />

    <br />

    <span style="font-size:16px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">By William Keene<br />

    <em>St. Louis Post-Dispatch</em></span></span><br />

    <br />

    <span style="font-size:19px;"><span style="font-family:Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif;">Following the state’s hottest year on record, some Missouri policymakers are looking to help the state adapt to a future in which weather extremes could well be the norm.<br />

    <br />

    There were 21 days of at least 100 degrees last year, and by the time the summer ended, the heat had killed 26 people in the St. Louis area.<br />

    <br />

    As a result, experts increasingly agree on the critical importance of cities and regions to plan adaptive responses to heat waves and drought. Adaptation addresses ways a community can prepare for the health and agricultural impacts of such weather extremes.<br />

    <br />

    For instance, the Missouri Health Department created a severe weather coordinator position to help protect residents from extreme heat.<br />

    <br />

    But many governments in the region, including those in the St. Louis area, have only just started thinking about how to adapt.<br />

    <br />

    Data from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration indicate average Midwestern temperatures have trended 1.5 degrees Fahrenheit higher over the last 120 years. In St. Louis, temperatures are 1 to 1.5 degrees hotter than the average from 1901 to 1960.<br />

    <br />

    The most obvious impact will be the rising heat’s direct effect on residents through heatstroke and dehydration.<br />

    <br />

    A study released last month estimated that deaths from extreme heat in St. Louis would rise to between 9 and 10 for each 100,000 people from fewer than 2 for each 100,000 residents now.<br />

    <br />

    So in one key nod to the need for adaptation, St. Louis started a Severe Weather Public Health Protection Program in part because of the likelihood of longer, more severe heat waves in an already hot city.<br />

    <br />

    Shontae Fluelen-Hays, who heads the Health Department division, helps reach out to the most at-risk people in the city – the elderly and the poor – and tells them about cooling centers and other resources.<br />

    <br />

    One of the tools the city is now using is a “functional-needs registry” that allows residents to share health and other personal information that first responders can use to plan for emergencies.<br />

    <br />

    The rising heat is spurring warnings of other, less-obvious effects.<br />

    <br />

    Environmental groups recently warned of an increase in tick populations and Lyme disease in Missouri due to warming temperatures.<br />

    <br />

    Other studies warn of longer allergy seasons and the deadly combination of heat and air pollution that increases respiratory illnesses.<br />

    <br />

    And foundation repair companies are inundated with calls from homeowners trying to prevent settlement damage as dry, cracked earth receded from their houses.<br />

    <br />

    “We know we’re going to get droughts,” said Ken Kunkel, a scientist with NOAA’s National Centers for Environmental Information and North Carolina State University. “That’s a certainty. They’re going to be more severe because of the higher temperature.”</span></span>
